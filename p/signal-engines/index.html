The Archetype

What Is a Signal Engine?

A product that aggregates fragmented data signals, applies domain-specific models, and outputs prescriptive optimization recommendations that directly increase customer revenue. Not a dashboard. A decision engine.

Aggregate

Pull together signals the customer can't synthesize themselves — across systems, time, and competitive context

Optimize

Apply domain-specific models to find patterns and opportunities invisible to any individual operator

Prescribe

Output specific actions: set this price, bid this amount, schedule this way, target these customers

Prove

Measure the dollar impact directly. Revenue up, margin improved, utilization increased. The product pays for itself

Why This Works as a Business

For the customer

Measurable ROI (often 5-20x subscription cost). Delivered to existing operations — no new capex, hiring, or process changes. Compounds over time as the model learns.

For the builder

Data moats deepen with each customer. Domain-specific models that general AI can't replicate. High willingness-to-pay because the product is a profit center, not a cost center.

Why Now?

Three converging forces make this the right time to build signal engines for blue-collar industries:

The data infrastructure finally exists

ServiceTitan, Bullhorn, Tekmetric, and others spent the last decade digitizing these industries. The data is now being captured and is accessible via APIs. Five years ago, it wasn't.

AI makes the models viable at SMB scale

Building a prescriptive optimization engine used to require a team of 20 data scientists. LLMs and modern ML infrastructure mean two people can build what used to take an entire department.

The operators are ready

Business coaching ($10-50K/year for trades, $500-1,500/month for auto repair) proves the demand for pricing and revenue optimization advice. The jump to software is natural and 10x cheaper.

Evaluation Framework

Scoring Model

Nine criteria that determine whether a signal engine opportunity is viable and high-potential. Each scored 1-5. High-weight criteria count double.

High Weight Signal Fragmentation ▶

How many disconnected data sources exist that the customer can't synthesize?

5 = Data scattered across 5+ systems with no integration. 1 = Most data already in one system. This is the core moat. If data is consolidated, the customer builds their own dashboards.

High Weight Optimization Complexity ▶

Is the problem genuinely hard for a human to solve, even with all the data?

5 = Multi-variable optimization with non-obvious tradeoffs. 1 = Simple enough for a spreadsheet. The best signal engines solve problems where the combinatorial space exceeds human capacity.

High Weight Value Measurability ▶

Can you directly measure the dollar impact?

5 = Output is a directly measurable financial metric (revenue, margin, utilization). 1 = Value is qualitative. Measurable ROI makes the product sell itself and enables value-based pricing.

High Weight Industry Fragmentation ▶

Are there many small operators who can't build this themselves?

5 = Thousands of independent operators. 1 = Highly consolidated, few players who can build in-house. Fragmented industries create large TAMs and word-of-mouth distribution.

Data Accessibility ▶

Can you actually get the data without prohibitive barriers?

5 = Available via public sources or standard APIs. 1 = Locked in proprietary systems or requires hardware. A brilliant model is worthless without data to feed it.

Margin Sensitivity ▶

How much does a small % improvement translate to in absolute dollars?

Sweet spot: thin margins AND high revenue per operator. A 3% margin improvement for a $2M company = $60K. That's 5-10x a $500/month subscription.

Workflow Embeddability ▶

Can recommendations be auto-executed via existing software?

5 = Can directly set prices or adjust schedules via integrations. 1 = Disconnected from daily operations. Auto-executed recommendations create stickiness and compound the data moat.

Competitive Landscape ▶

How crowded is the space?

5 = No meaningful player doing multi-signal optimization. 1 = Well-funded incumbents. Being first matters because data moats are self-reinforcing.

Bootstrappability ▶

Can this reach revenue without significant upfront capital?

5 = Start with publicly available data, lightweight integrations, small pilot cohort. 1 = Requires massive infrastructure or regulatory approvals. The path to first revenue must be short.

The Sera distinction ▶

Why margin protection is not the same as revenue optimization

Sera ($34M raised) is often cited as "smart pricing" for trades. The distinction is fundamental:

Sera says: "Your cost to operate is $X/hour, so charge at least $Y to hit your margin target." This is a cost-plus floor.

A signal engine says: "Given current capacity, seasonality, customer context, and your historical close rates — charge $Z to maximize revenue." This is demand-aware optimization.

Sera ensures you don't lose money. A signal engine maximizes how much money you make. One sets a floor. The other finds the ceiling.

Why HVAC first: Highest ticket values in trades (~$8K installs), most seasonal variability (largest optimization surface), most analytically curious operators (high tickets make every pricing decision material).

MVP: Dynamic job pricing recommendations via ServiceTitan API integration. Pull job history, schedule/capacity, customer location, weather, and historical close rates. Output: recommended price for each new job.

Distribution: ServiceTitan partner marketplace → trade association conferences (ACCA, PHCC) → buying groups and Facebook communities. The trades community is tight-knit.

Pricing: $500-1,500/month. Value delivered: $50-200K/year per company.

Coaching industry validation: Nexstar Network, Service Nation, BDR charge $10-50K/year for memberships that include manual pricing advice. A software product does this data-driven and continuously at 1/10th the cost.

However: (a) their DNA is workflow tools, not data science, (b) prescriptive optimization is a fundamentally different competency, (c) they haven't moved in this direction despite years of data. More likely an acquisition target than a builder.

Operator adoption: Trade owners are resistant to new tech. Position as "AI-powered pricing coach" not "automated dynamic pricing." The coaching wrapper may be as important as the algorithm.

Why the dental model translates directly ▶

Structural similarities between dental and vet practices

Dental and veterinary practices share almost identical business structures:

  • Fragmented independent practices (35K+ vet, 200K+ dental)
  • Fee-for-service model
  • Similar ticket sizes ($200-$2,000 per visit)
  • Case acceptance / compliance challenges
  • Scheduling density directly drives revenue
  • Practice owners are clinicians, not business operators

Mars/IDEXX ownership creates a positioning advantage. VetSuccess (Mars) and PetDesk (IDEXX) are owned by large incumbents, which creates distrust among independent practices. An independent optimization platform would be welcomed.

Consolidation creates a second buyer. Corporate consolidators (Mars, NVA, VCA, Thrive) are rapidly buying independent practices. They need optimization tools to extract value post-acquisition. You'd sell to both independents (compete better) and corporate groups (optimize acquisitions).

Go-to-market ▶

PIMS integration, case acceptance optimization, vet conferences

Beachhead: Independent vet practices using Cornerstone or Avimark PIMS. Build integration to pull appointment data, treatment plans, billing, and patient history.

MVP: Case acceptance optimization — score declined treatment plans by predicted acceptance probability, recommend optimal pricing and follow-up timing. Scheduling density — identify empty slots and recommend reactivation targets to fill them.

Pricing: $500-1,500/month. A practice unlocking $150-300K/year easily justifies this.

Distribution: VMX and WVC conferences, vet practice management groups, PIMS partner ecosystems.

Key risks ▶

PIMS integration complexity, IDEXX data moat, shrinking independent TAM

PIMS fragmentation: Vet PIMS landscape is fragmented (Cornerstone, Avimark, eVetPractice, Shepherd, Digitail, Provet). Need 5-6 integrations for meaningful coverage. Significant engineering effort but defensible once built.

IDEXX: Controls dominant PIMS (Cornerstone) and diagnostics data. Could build analytics. But IDEXX has been slow to innovate in software and focuses on diagnostics revenue.

Consolidation shrinking TAM: Independent practices are being acquired by corporate groups. But ~25K+ independents remain in the US, and corporate groups are also potential buyers. The TAM is evolving, not disappearing.

Untouched optimization surfaces ▶

Five capabilities no one offers

Variable parts markup: Today: static tiers by cost range. Opportunity: AI-driven markup by part type, availability, competitive positioning, and customer segment.

Labor rate by service type: Today: one flat rate (maybe 2-3). Opportunity: optimal rates for diagnostics vs. maintenance vs. mechanical vs. specialty.

Upsell prioritization: Today: DVI shows everything wrong, advisor decides. Opportunity: score upsells by margin potential × approval probability × vehicle data.

Bay utilization: Today: shown as a metric, never optimized. Opportunity: schedule optimization to maximize revenue per bay-hour.

Customer reactivation: Today: time-based ("hasn't visited in 6 months"). Opportunity: value-optimized by CLV, predicted service needs, and margin potential.

Tekmetric integration, ARO optimization positioning

Beachhead: Independent shops using Tekmetric (API-first, growing fast, progressive owners). Build integration, demonstrate GP per RO improvement in 60 days.

Positioning: The industry already obsesses over ARO (Average Repair Order). Frame as "ARO optimization" — language they already use and care about.

Distribution: Shop owner communities (20 groups), Facebook groups, AAPEX and SEMA trade shows.

Biggest risk isn't competition — it's adoption. Shop owners are notoriously resistant to new tech. The product may need to feel like a "pricing coach" rather than "automated pricing software."

The Approach

One Platform, Multiple Markets

Build the optimization engine once. Deploy into each market with a domain-specific front end and data model. Start with one, prove it, expand.

The core optimization math — multi-signal aggregation, prescriptive recommendations, ROI measurement — is the same across all five markets. What changes is the data sources, the domain model, and the UI. That's a platform, not five separate products.

The Shared Platform

Data Ingestion Layer

Connectors for industry-specific software (ServiceTitan, Bullhorn, Tekmetric, etc.). Each market adds a new connector to the same pipeline.

Optimization Engine

The core ML/AI models for multi-variable optimization. Price elasticity, demand forecasting, capacity optimization. Shared math, domain-specific features.

Recommendation Delivery

The interface layer that surfaces prescriptive actions. Embeds into existing workflows via API, dashboard, or push notifications.

ROI Measurement

Closed-loop tracking that proves the value. "Last month, following our recommendations generated $X in incremental revenue." This is the renewal engine.

Portfolio Sequencing

Start with one market to build the base platform and prove the model. Then expand.

Phase 1: Prove It

One market, 10 pilot customers, 90 days to measurable ROI

Build the core platform and one market-specific deployment. Demonstrate revenue improvement for a handful of customers. Create case studies. Reach $10-20K MRR.

Phase 2: Scale the First Market

Partner marketplace, conference presence, 50-100 customers

Case studies drive word-of-mouth. Partner marketplace listing (ServiceTitan, Bullhorn, etc.) drives inbound. Data moat starts compounding. Reach $50-100K MRR.

Phase 3: Second Market

New connector, new domain model, same engine

The platform is proven. Adding a new market means building a data connector, training the domain model, and designing the UI. The core optimization engine, recommendation delivery, and ROI measurement already exist.

Phase 4+: Portfolio

Multiple markets, compounding data moats, real leverage

Each market has its own data moat and revenue stream. Some will work better than others. The winners compound. The portfolio diversifies risk and maximizes the chance of finding a breakout market.
